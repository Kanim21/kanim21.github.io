case study · hospital-ai-orchestrator

Software that plans a patient's discharge — but a person always approves the risky part.

Hospitals move patients between beds, transport, and discharge constantly — usually routine, occasionally dangerous if rushed. This project builds an assistant that handles the checklists, then freezes and hands control to a person the moment a decision could actually hurt someone.

# self-audit This system checked its own work, found a real privacy leak in how it logged a reviewer's note, and fixed it the same day — before anyone outside the project ever saw it. Open the redaction guardrail in the diagram below for the full story.

the idea, without the jargon

$ how to think about this

Picture a hospital shift coordinator working a busy ward. They don't discharge patients, manage beds, or book transport themselves — they hand each case to the right specialist, in the right order, and never let two specialists touch the same record at once.

Every specialist gets information the same way: through a pharmacy counter, not a back door. They hand over a request — "show me this patient's chart" — instead of walking into the records room and reading whatever they like. That's the only way any of them touch real hospital data.

And when a decision could actually hurt someone — moving a patient into an already-occupied bed, or transporting someone with a specialised need and no sign-off — the coordinator stops entirely and calls over a real person. Nothing risky happens until someone accountable says yes.

what could go wrong

$ failure modes, handled on purpose

If this happensWhat the system does
Someone asks about a patient who doesn't exist A safety net catches the failure and stops cleanly with a labelled error — no crash, no half-finished bed change.
A reviewer's note contains a name, email, or phone number The note passes through the same redaction filter as everything else before it's ever logged — the fix for the project's one real leak.
The target bed is already occupied by someone else Flagged high risk immediately. The workflow pauses and waits for a person instead of overriding the bed.
A transfer needs supervisor sign-off that isn't on file Flagged high risk and sent to a human. Nothing moves without that sign-off, or an explicit approval instead.
No transport staff are available on the unit Flagged medium risk — enough to pause the workflow rather than dispatch into a staffing gap.
# what this project is not: a certified clinical system. Every patient is fictional, the privacy filter is explicitly prototype-grade rather than a certified de-identification tool, records live in plain files with no real access control, and "risk" is a fixed checklist, not a clinician's judgement.
# glossary — 10 terms, click to expand
FHIR
The standard shape hospital computer systems use to describe a patient's record, so different systems can understand each other.
PHI
Protected Health Information — any detail that could identify a patient, like a name or health card number.
PHIPA
Ontario's health-privacy law. It requires collecting and showing only the minimum identifying information necessary.
MCP
Model Context Protocol — a fixed, limited set of doors an AI is allowed to use to touch real data.
LangGraph
A way of writing a multi-step process as an explicit flowchart, so you can see exactly what happens next.
Human-in-the-loop
A checkpoint where a workflow pauses and waits for a real person's decision before continuing.
Redaction
Automatically blacking out identifying information from text before it's stored or shown.
Audit trail
A permanent, add-only record of every action taken, so anything can be reviewed later.
Error boundary
A safety wrapper that catches a failure and turns it into a clean, labelled stop instead of a crash.
Checkpointing
Saving a workflow's exact state so it can pause — sometimes for a long time — and resume later.
